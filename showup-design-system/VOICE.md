# ShowUp — VOICE.md

## Brand Voice Character

ShowUp speaks like someone who has done the reading and knows the exits. It doesn't over-explain — it trusts the user to keep up, because the user is risking something real by being here. When it's warm, the warmth feels earned rather than performed. When it's cold, the coldness is protective, not cruel. Every word is weighed against the reality that the person reading it may be in danger, may be watched, and does not have time for nonsense.

## Tone Dimensions

### Formality
Conversational but composed. Uses contractions freely. Avoids jargon and buzzwords. No "leverage," "synergy," "optimize," or "streamline." The vocabulary is plain but precise — like someone explaining a plan in a loud bar, leaning in close so they won't be overheard.

### Personality
Dry wit is welcome. Forced cheerfulness is not. The tone should make you feel capable, not managed. Sarcasm is allowed but never at the user's expense. The brand can be wry about systems and authority; it is never wry about risk or safety.

### Empathy
Always present, never precious. It acknowledges difficulty without wallowing in it. "Protests are exhausting" is empathetic. "We know this is hard and we're so sorry you're going through this" is precious and condescending.

### Directness
Says the thing. No preamble. No "Great question!" No "We're so excited to announce." No "In order to..." Just the information, the action, and the consequence.

### Confidence
States things. Doesn't hedge constantly. Qualifies only when it matters. "You are anonymous" not "We believe you may be anonymous in most circumstances." The user is trusting ShowUp with their safety; the brand must sound like it knows what it's doing.

## Tone in Context

### Onboarding
Welcoming without being effusive. Gives orientation, not a tutorial. Trusts the user to figure things out. The first screen says what the app does in one sentence, then gets out of the way. No swipe-through explanations. No tooltips on first launch.

### Empty States
Honest about the emptiness. Maybe slightly wry. Always gives the user a clear next step. "No events nearby. Pan the map or check back later." Not "Oops! Looks like there's nothing here yet! 🎉"

### Error Messages
Describes what happened without assigning blame. Explains the next step. Does not apologize profusely — a brief "Something went wrong" is more honest than "We're so sorry for this terrible experience!" Errors are facts, not relationships.

### Success States
Brief celebration, then get out of the way. One sentence max. "You're in." "Message sent." "Event created." No confetti, no exclamation points, no "Awesome!"

### Loading / Processing
Honest about the wait. One sentence of context if relevant. Never filler phrases. "Loading map tiles..." not "Just a moment while we prepare your experience..."

### Destructive Actions / Confirmations
Calm and clear. Describes exactly what will happen. Doesn't catastrophize, but doesn't minimize either. "Leave this event? You won't be able to rejoin without a new code." Not "Are you sure?!" and not "This will delete everything forever."

## Vocabulary

### Words and Phrases ShowUp Uses

- Presence
- Signal
- Zone
- Channel
- Token
- Verify
- Join / Leave (not "Subscribe / Unsubscribe")
- Nearby
- Active / Ended
- Collectives
- Flash (for sudden, time-limited events)
- Burner
- Coordinates
- Encrypted
- Mesh
- Dead drop

### Words and Phrases ShowUp Never Uses

- Users (say "people" or be specific)
- Experience
- Journey
- Engagement
- Content
- Community (as a hollow noun — only if referring to a specific group)
- Optimized
- Seamless
- Frictionless
- Delight
- Exciting
- Awesome
- Oops
- Sorry (unless we actually caused harm)
- Please (in instructions — "Enter your code" not "Please enter your code")
- Leverage
- Synergy
- Streamline
- Onboarding (use "setup" or skip it entirely)

### Sentence Length
Short and declarative by default. Longer only when a concept genuinely needs the space. Most UI copy should be one line. Explanations should be one paragraph. If it needs more, it might need to be broken up.

### Punctuation Character

- **Periods:** Default. Every sentence ends with a period, even one-word sentences.
- **Serial comma:** Used. Precision in lists matters.
- **Em dashes:** Used for abrupt insertions or corrections. "Your token is valid — don't share it."
- **Ellipsis:** Used only for ongoing processes or truncated text. Never for dramatic pause.
- **Exclamation points:** Almost never. If used, it signals genuine alarm: "Leave now!" — not enthusiasm.
- **Question marks:** Used sparingly. ShowUp doesn't ask the user things it should know. "Leave this event?" is acceptable. "How are you feeling today?" is not.
